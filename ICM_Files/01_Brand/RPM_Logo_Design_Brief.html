01 — About the Brand

What is Roaring Pines Motor Club?

The Project

Roaring Pines Motor Club (RPM) is a private, all-season motorsports destination in Northeast Florida — 480 acres built around a FIA-rated, multi-configurable road course with direct fly-in access via an adjacent airport.

The club is for performance car owners who want a real track — not a weekend autocross or a track-day rental. Permanent garage condos, a boutique hotel, clubhouse, restaurant, and motorsports museum make this a full lifestyle destination.

The Team

Founded by Scott Lagasse Sr. and Scott Lagasse Jr. — a father-son duo with decades of professional NASCAR and motorsport experience. The club is built by drivers, for drivers. That authenticity is central to the brand.

The Market Position

There is no private motor club at this tier currently operating in Florida. Roaring Pines is building the category, not competing within it.

One sentence: Roaring Pines is where serious performance car owners go when they're done pretending public roads are enough.

02 — The Logo Concept

RPM — The Double Meaning

The primary brand identifier is RPM. It works on two levels simultaneously — and both must feel intentional:

R·P·M

Roaring Pines
Motor Club

The initials. Clean, memorable, ownable. Works as a standalone mark without explanation.

R·P·M

Revolutions
Per Minute

The engine language. Instantly understood by anyone who drives. No translation needed.

The logo does not need to explain either meaning. Anyone who sees "RPM" at a motor club already knows both. The design should let the letters do the work.

The Core Brand Line

"For Drivers. By Drivers."

This is the brand's north star. Every design decision should feel like it was made by someone who has driven a performance car on a real track — not by someone who looked up "luxury motor club" on Google Images.

03 — Design Territories

Three Directions to Explore

We are open to multiple interpretations. Below are three distinct territories. Designers are encouraged to explore one or more — but each direction must feel distinctly different from the others.

A
Typographic Power

Pure Letterform

The RPM letters are the entire mark. No icon, no emblem — just the three letters treated as pure visual architecture. Requires exceptional font choice and spacing execution. Think: the strength of a Porsche wordmark or Ferrari script — identity through type alone.

  • Custom or highly modified letterforms
  • Extreme weight, precision spacing
  • The letters themselves carry the brand's personality
  • Scales from 8px favicon to 10-foot wall sign
B
Badge / Emblem

Motor Club Crest

An enclosed badge or crest containing RPM — the traditional language of private motor clubs, racing teams, and exclusive institutions. Heritage and permanence. Think: FIA badges, Le Mans team crests, Laguna Seca marks — timeless, not retro.

  • Shield, oval, circle, or custom enclosure shape
  • RPM as the dominant interior element
  • "Roaring Pines Motor Club" on the badge perimeter
  • Must avoid looking dated or generic
C
Symbol + Wordmark

Icon & Letters

A symbol or icon that pairs with the RPM wordmark. The symbol should reference motorsport or the specific Roaring Pines concept — without being literal or clichéd. Think: an abstracted track element, a tachometer needle, a motion-inspired form that reads as mark before it reads as meaning.

  • Icon must work independently of the wordmark
  • Avoid: steering wheels, checkered flags, tire marks
  • Geometric, minimal, precise
  • The concept should reward a second look

Primary Usage

The logo must be designed in these two primary configurations:

  • Dark version — white/light on black ground
  • Light version — black on white/cream ground
  • One-color version — pure black (for embossing, foil, etc.)
  • One-color white reverse (for dark substrates)

Colour Philosophy

Red is earned, not decorative. It appears once — as the accent that carries meaning, like a tachometer's redline sector. It should never dominate. Black and white carry the mark. Red punctuates it.

If using gold: it replaces red as the prestige accent, not an addition to it.

05 — Typography Direction

Type Character

The typeface choice is the single most important design decision for this mark. The letters RPM must feel like they were engineered, not decorated.

Character We're After

  • Confident and heavy without being crude
  • Precise — as if the letterforms were machined to tolerance
  • Timeless — will read the same in 30 years as today
  • Works at both massive scale (building signage) and small scale (embossed card)
  • Custom modifications or letterfit are welcome — this should not look like an off-the-shelf font application

Secondary Text Treatment

"Roaring Pines Motor Club" appears as supporting text — never competing with RPM. It should be tight, precise, and technical in character. Wide letter-spacing preferred. Think instrument panel labels, not marketing headlines.

  • Light to regular weight only
  • Generous letter-spacing (tracking)
  • All caps or small caps preferred
  • Must recede visually behind RPM

Typography test: Cover the subtext. The RPM mark alone should instantly communicate premium, performance, and precision — with zero supporting context.

06 — Mood & References

Look & Feel

These are directional references — not targets to copy. They illustrate the emotional territory and standard of craft we're aiming for.

Motor Racing Heritage

FIA Crest & Formula 1 Team Identities

The precision and authority of motorsport's governing bodies. Scuderia Ferrari, Red Bull Racing, AMG. Official, serious, built on decades of credibility.

Private Clubs

The Thermal Club / Augusta National

Exclusive, understated, permanent. These brands don't shout — they simply exist with authority. Membership is implied, not advertised.

German Performance Marques

Porsche / AMG Wordmarks

Typography as identity. The wordmark IS the brand. Precise, engineered letterforms that feel purpose-built rather than designed.

Racing Circuits

Nürburgring / Circuit de la Sarthe

Track identities that carry weight and history. Bold, direct, built for permanence — not trend cycles.

❌ Avoid

  • Generic "luxury" clichés (thin serifs, script fonts, ornamental filigree)
  • Checkered flags, steering wheels, tire marks
  • Speed lines or motion blur effects
  • Bright, saturated reds or flashy color combinations
  • Anything that looks like a car dealership logo
  • Gradients, drop shadows, lens flares
  • Cartoony or illustrative approaches
  • Logos that need color to communicate — must work in black first

✓ Pursue

  • Marks that feel engineered, not decorated
  • Geometry that rewards close inspection
  • Confident use of negative space
  • A single subtle reference to motorsport that only enthusiasts catch
  • Balance between heritage weight and modern precision
  • Marks that work equally well engraved on metal and displayed on a screen
  • Typography that feels custom, not off-the-shelf
  • Simplicity — if something can be removed, remove it

08 — Applications Context

Where This Logo Will Live

Design for these surfaces from the start. A mark that only works on a white screen is not the right mark.

Digital

  • Website header and favicon
  • Social media profile image
  • Email signature
  • Digital marketing assets
  • Presentation decks

Physical / Signage

  • Garage door + pit lane signage
  • Track entry monument sign
  • Clubhouse exterior lettering
  • Vehicle livery / decal
  • Member ID card / badge

Merchandise & Print

  • Embroidered cap / apparel
  • Laser-engraved metal plate
  • Letterhead + stationery
  • Member welcome package
  • Dupont Registry advertisement

Key test: If the logo works laser-engraved on a brushed aluminum garage plaque and also works as a social media avatar, it's the right logo. If it only works in one of those contexts, it needs more work.
